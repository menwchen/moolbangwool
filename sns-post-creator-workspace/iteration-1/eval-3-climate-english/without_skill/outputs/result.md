## Facebook

### Post 1

**The Climate Crisis and Plastic Pollution Are Two Sides of the Same Coin**

When we think of climate change, we picture smokestacks and car exhaust. When we think of ocean pollution, we picture sea turtles tangled in plastic bags. But these two crises are far more connected than most people realize.

Here's how the cycle works:

1. **Plastic is made from fossil fuels.** The production of plastic accounts for roughly 3.4% of global greenhouse gas emissions — and that number is rising as production ramps up.

2. **Ocean plastic releases greenhouse gases.** When plastic breaks down in seawater, it releases methane and ethylene. Warmer waters caused by climate change speed up this degradation, creating a dangerous feedback loop.

3. **Microplastics harm carbon-absorbing ecosystems.** Phytoplankton and other marine organisms that absorb CO2 are being affected by microplastic contamination, potentially weakening the ocean's ability to act as a carbon sink.

4. **Climate change worsens plastic distribution.** Stronger storms, rising sea levels, and changing ocean currents are spreading plastic waste into previously untouched ecosystems, including the deep ocean and Arctic regions.

The bottom line: we can't fight climate change without tackling plastic pollution, and vice versa.

**What can you do?**
- Reduce single-use plastic in your daily life
- Support policies that limit plastic production and promote circular economies
- Choose products with minimal or recyclable packaging
- Advocate for corporate accountability in plastic waste

Every piece of plastic you refuse is a small victory for both the ocean and the climate. Share this post if you believe these two fights are one and the same.

### Post 2

**Did you know? The plastic in our oceans is making climate change worse.**

Most of us know that plastic pollution harms marine life. But fewer people know that plastic in the ocean actively contributes to global warming.

As sunlight and warm seawater break plastic down into smaller and smaller particles, those fragments release greenhouse gases — including methane, one of the most potent heat-trapping gases on the planet.

And here's the cruel irony: as our planet warms, ocean temperatures rise, which causes plastic to degrade even faster, releasing even more emissions. It's a self-reinforcing cycle that's getting harder to stop.

At the same time, the global plastics industry continues to expand. Plastic production has doubled over the past 20 years and is projected to triple by 2060 if current trends continue.

This isn't just an ocean problem. It's a climate problem. It's a health problem. And it demands a systemic response.

The good news? Momentum is building. Nations around the world have been working toward a Global Plastics Treaty, and more cities and countries are banning single-use plastics every year.

But policy alone isn't enough. It takes all of us — consumers, businesses, and governments — working together.
